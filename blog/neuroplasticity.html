Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Understanding neuroplasticity helps people to understand that their brains can change and develop at any age and that nothing is a static given. Once this idea is grasped it can free someone from thinking that they are unable to change or grow due to their current situation being set in stone. For instance, believing that you cannot acquire new intelligence or skills because of your age is an example of this mindset. In reality, you can transform your brain by tackling challenges and engaging in new activities until they become second nature. By learning new things, you create new neural pathways, effectively reshaping your brain through effort and practice. There are many ways to increase your neuroplasticity, some examples of these can be through diet, enough rest, not overloading your brain, expanding your vocabulary or learning a new language and doing mind drills or games. Keeping your brain fresh and not overworked, and thus not performing to its maximum, is critical but so is keeping it engaged with new challenges and problems to overcome. Growth Mindset

The two different mindsets put forth in this argument are a fixed mindset and a growth mindset. A growth mindset is a way of thinking about ourselves that can drastically alter the way we learn. Those with a growth mindset see challenges and failures as possibilities to increase their own intelligence, they have a desire to learn and, when encountering problems, embrace these as chances to reach further heights of achievement. Those with a fixed mindset, on the other hand, avoid challenges to then avoid potential failures. If, having completed a problem, would not opt for a more technical one, given the chance, and feel threatened by the success of others. They see intelligence as a static given, rather than a changeable entity based on how much effort has been put in. Reading about the growth mindset has been very interesting and makes me second guess the way I think about things and encounter problems. I’m not sure what camp I fall into currently but love the idea of being able to alter one’s mindset/intelligence/psychology. I’ll try and integrate these ideas into my learning, and further into my life, by not dwelling on my failures and trying my best to enjoy the challenges of things unknown to me (even if it’s JS loops!). Much of the work done on this idea is by Stanford psychologist Carol Dweck, you can watch this TED talk below on ‘The Power of Believing That You Can Improve’. I found it very interesting and a good overview on the growth mindset.

Blending with my learning plan and strategies

I really enjoyed learning about neuroplasticity and the growth mindset. The two ideas work very well together and offer a real freedom to us, especially while in the midst of studying. It makes the future seem like more of an adventure knowing that you could keep expanding your mind exponentially and with no real way to know what will come, so long as you keep challenging your brain. I’ll take these ideas into my learning strategy by way of my approach to challenges and problems encountered. Also I’d like to strive to see what other things I can add to my daily life to keep my mind growing and evolving.
